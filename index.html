Feature: Maritime Research & Operations

The Hull Truth: New Research Reveals the Real Cost of Marine Growth

By Mathew Harvey
Biofouling Manager, Franmarine

For decades, maritime operators have faced a persistent challenge: quantifying the exact relationship between hull fouling and operational costs. While the connection between marine growth and increased fuel consumption has been well understood in principle, precise measurements have remained elusive. Now, groundbreaking research from the University of Melbourne, employing advanced underwater scanning technology, has delivered the hard data the industry has been waiting for: even minimal hull fouling can impose significant and measurable fuel penalties that directly impact the bottom line [1].

Consider this: a vessel operating with heavy calcareous fouling (FR5) faces a staggering 193% increase in frictional resistance. For the 93-meter Coral Adventurer cruise ship studied by the University of Melbourne team, this translated to an additional $1,273 per hour in fuel costs and 1.8 tonnes of extra CO₂ emissions per hour when maintaining cruise speed [1]. These are not theoretical projections—they are measured realities that demand immediate industry attention.

Measuring the Unmeasurable

The 2024 University of Melbourne and Franmarine study has accomplished what many thought impossible: establishing a clear, quantitative link between surface roughness and operational efficiency. Using sophisticated underwater scanning technology (AQUAMARS), researchers have mapped the direct correlation between fouling ratings and fuel consumption. The findings are compelling: even light slime (FR1) can increase fuel consumption by up to 10%, while moderate slime (FR2) pushes this penalty to 20–30% [1].

Figure 1: AQUAMARS technology captures sub-millimeter surface roughness data on the Rio Tinto tugboat hull during the University of Melbourne study.
Key Research Findings
  • Light biofilm (FR1): Increases fuel consumption by up to 10% [1]
  • Moderate slime (FR2): Results in 20–30% higher fuel consumption [1]
  • Heavy calcareous growth (FR4–FR5): Can drive fuel costs up by 45–60% [1]
  • Speed factor: Drag penalties increase dramatically at higher velocities due to the cubic relationship between speed and resistance [1]

The Operational Impact

These findings transform what was once a maintenance issue into a critical operational consideration. For the Rio Tinto tugboat examined in the December 2024 study, moderate fouling resulted in a 125% increase in skin friction coefficient and a 44.2% increase in total resistance at 13 knots. This equates to approximately $955 per hour in additional fuel costs and 1.3 tonnes per hour of extra CO₂ emissions [2].

With shipping accounting for approximately 3% of global CO₂ emissions, the implications extend beyond fuel bills to regulatory compliance and environmental stewardship. The International Maritime Organization's tightening emissions intensity requirements (CII) make hull performance an increasingly crucial factor in operational planning [3].

Figure 2: Operating costs and CO₂ emissions increase exponentially with speed for fouled hulls, as demonstrated by the University of Melbourne research.

The Science Behind the Numbers

The AQUAMARS (Advanced Quality Underwater Mapping and Analysis for Rough Surfaces) technology represents a paradigm shift in hull assessment. This diver-operated device uses eight high-resolution cameras to capture simultaneous images, creating 3D surface reconstructions with sub-millimeter accuracy. The system measures critical parameters including equivalent sandgrain roughness (ks), which directly correlates to drag penalties [2].

What makes this research particularly valuable is its translation of complex fluid dynamics into practical operational metrics. The team's analysis revealed that fouling affects two primary resistance components: frictional resistance (which follows a cubic relationship with speed) and wave-making resistance (which increases even more rapidly). Understanding this relationship allows operators to make informed decisions about cleaning schedules based on actual performance data rather than arbitrary timelines.

Figure 3: AQUAMARS technology produces detailed 3D surface maps, revealing the heterogeneous nature of hull fouling and enabling precise drag calculations.

Evidence-Based Solutions

The research points to several strategies for maintaining optimal hull performance, which have been validated through both laboratory studies and full-scale vessel measurements [1, 2, 4]:

  • Proactive Monitoring: Regular ROV and diver inspections can identify fouling at its earliest stages, when intervention is most cost-effective. The data shows that early detection can prevent the exponential growth of both marine organisms and operational costs.
  • Strategic Cleaning: The research validates that gentle hull cleaning effectively manages biofouling without compromising coating integrity. The Coral Adventurer study demonstrated that timely intervention could save over $3 million annually in fuel costs alone [1].
  • Comprehensive Approach: Combining propeller maintenance with hull cleaning yields compounded efficiency gains, as documented in both naval and commercial vessel studies [4].
  • Performance Tracking: Continuous monitoring of speed-power relationships provides early warning of efficiency losses, enabling data-driven maintenance decisions.

The Economic Imperative

The University of Melbourne study hasn't just confirmed industry suspicions—it has provided a robust scientific framework for quantifying losses and justifying investments in hull maintenance. For vessels operating on tight margins, these insights transform hull upkeep from a periodic necessity into a continuous optimization strategy.

The numbers speak volumes: a typical commercial vessel operating 200 days annually could face additional fuel costs exceeding $2-3 million per year with moderate to heavy fouling. When multiplied across global fleets, the economic impact reaches tens of billions of dollars annually—costs that are ultimately passed to consumers through higher shipping rates.

Moreover, the environmental implications cannot be ignored. With each percentage point of increased resistance translating directly to higher emissions, hull fouling management becomes a critical component of any serious decarbonization strategy. The research demonstrates that maintaining clean hulls could reduce the global shipping industry's carbon footprint by millions of tonnes annually.

Looking Forward

As the maritime industry navigates toward a lower-carbon future, the University of Melbourne's research provides both a wake-up call and a roadmap. The technology and methodologies developed through these studies offer operators the tools to make informed, data-driven decisions about hull maintenance.

The research team continues to expand their database, with ongoing studies examining different vessel types, operating conditions, and fouling patterns. Future developments include real-time monitoring systems and predictive models that will enable even more precise optimization of cleaning schedules.

The message is clear: in an era of rising fuel costs and stringent environmental regulations, maintaining optimal hull condition is not merely about vessel upkeep—it's about sustainable operations, both environmentally and economically. The path to efficiency, quite literally, depends on what happens beneath the waterline.

Putting Research into Practice

To make these insights actionable, an online Fouling Cost Calculator has been developed and availble in the Tools section of www.marinestream.com.au . This is based on the University of Melbourne research findings, allows operators to input vessel parameters and fouling conditions to receive immediate estimates of fuel penalties and emissions impacts. By translating complex fluid dynamics into practical cost projections, the calculator bridges the gap between academic research and operational decision-making. This calculator will become more accurate and useful over time as more vessels are scanned.
